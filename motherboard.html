Motherboard

The central nervous system of your computer

What is a Motherboard?

The motherboard is the main printed circuit board (PCB) in a computer that connects all the components together. It's the backbone of your system, allowing communication between the CPU, RAM, storage, GPU, and other peripherals. The motherboard determines what components are compatible and how they interact with each other.

Key Motherboard Components

CPU Socket

The physical interface where the processor connects. Different sockets support different CPU generations and brands.

RAM Slots

Slots for memory modules. Most boards have 2-4 slots supporting dual or quad channel configurations.

Chipset

The motherboard's traffic controller that manages data flow between components. Determines features and capabilities.

Expansion Slots

PCIe slots for graphics cards, sound cards, and other expansion cards. Different sizes (x1, x4, x8, x16).

Storage Connectors

SATA ports for traditional drives and M.2 slots for modern SSDs.

Power Connectors

24-pin main power and 8/4-pin CPU power connectors from the PSU.

Motherboard Form Factors

ATX (12" x 9.6")

Standard full-size motherboard. Most expansion slots and features. Ideal for gaming and enthusiast builds.

  • Most expansion slots
  • Best for multiple GPUs
  • Requires larger case

Micro-ATX (9.6" x 9.6")

Smaller version of ATX. Good balance of size and features. Popular for mid-range builds.

  • Fewer expansion slots
  • Compact but capable
  • Good for most users

Mini-ITX (6.7" x 6.7")

Smallest consumer form factor. Limited expansion but perfect for compact builds.

  • Minimal expansion options
  • Very compact
  • Perfect for small form factor PCs

Chipset Comparison

Intel Chipsets

H-Series (Entry)

Basic features, limited overclocking, suitable for office and basic use.

B-Series (Mid-range)

Good balance of features, some overclocking support, mainstream choice.

Z-Series (High-end)

Full overclocking support, most features, enthusiast and gaming focused.

AMD Chipsets

A-Series (Entry)

Basic features, budget-oriented, good for basic computing.

B-Series (Mid-range)

Mainstream features, good value, popular choice.

X-Series (High-end)

Premium features, full overclocking, enthusiast grade.

Important Features to Consider

VRM Quality

Voltage Regulator Module quality affects CPU power delivery and overclocking stability.

Connectivity

USB ports, networking (WiFi, Ethernet), audio quality, and other I/O options.

BIOS/UEFI

Modern UEFI interfaces make system configuration easier and more user-friendly.

Build Quality

PCB layers, component quality, and overall construction affect reliability.

Quick Facts

  • Motherboard determines CPU compatibility
  • Form factor affects case choice
  • VRMs affect overclocking potential
  • BIOS updates can add features

Choosing a Motherboard

  • Match CPU socket type
  • Consider form factor
  • Check RAM support
  • Ensure enough expansion slots
  • Verify storage connections
