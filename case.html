Computer Case

The protective home for your components

What is a Computer Case?

A computer case (also called a chassis, tower, or enclosure) is the protective shell that houses all the computer components. It provides physical protection, organizes components, manages airflow for cooling, and offers connectivity for peripherals. The case also contributes to the overall aesthetics of your computer build.

Case Form Factors

Full Tower

Largest case type, offering maximum space for components and cooling solutions.

  • Height: 22-27 inches
  • Supports E-ATX motherboards
  • Multiple GPU support
  • Extensive cooling options
  • Heavy and expensive

Mid Tower

Most popular case size, balancing space and footprint. Good for most builds.

  • Height: 17-20 inches
  • Supports ATX/Micro-ATX
  • Good component compatibility
  • Reasonable size and weight
  • Best value for most users

Mini Tower

Compact case for smaller builds with limited space requirements.

  • Height: 12-16 inches
  • Supports Micro-ATX/Mini-ITX
  • Compact footprint
  • Limited component options
  • Good for office/gaming setups

Small Form Factor (SFF)

Very compact cases for portable or space-constrained builds.

  • Various shapes and sizes
  • Mini-ITX only
  • Extreme space optimization
  • Limited cooling options
  • Challenging to build in

Case Materials

Steel

Most common material. Durable, affordable, but heavy. Often used for budget and mid-range cases.

  • Very durable
  • Affordable
  • Heavy
  • Can dampen noise

Aluminum

Lighter and more premium than steel. Used in high-end and enthusiast cases.

  • Lightweight
  • Premium feel
  • More expensive
  • Can dent more easily

Tempered Glass

Popular for side panels to showcase components. Heavy but looks premium.

  • Excellent aesthetics
  • Shows off components
  • Heavy
  • Can break if dropped

Acrylic

Lighter alternative to glass. More scratch-prone but cheaper.

  • Lightweight
  • Affordable
  • Scratches easily
  • Can yellow over time

Important Case Features

Airflow Design

Case design affects cooling performance. Look for mesh panels, fan mounts, and good ventilation paths.

Cable Management

Good cable management features include routing holes, tie-down points, and hidden compartments.

Drive Bays

Spaces for storage drives. Modern cases often have fewer 3.5" bays and more 2.5" mounts.

Expansion Slots

Number of PCIe slot openings at the back. Important for multiple GPUs or expansion cards.

I/O Panel

Front connectivity including USB ports, audio jacks, and sometimes Type-C or fan controllers.

Dust Filters

Magnetic or removable filters that prevent dust from entering the case while allowing airflow.

Case Layout Considerations

Traditional Layout

PSU at top, motherboard horizontal. Common in older and budget cases.

Modern Layout

PSU at bottom, motherboard horizontal. Better for airflow and cable management.

Inverted Layout

Motherboard reversed, showing components through side panel. Unique aesthetic.

Dual Chamber

Separate compartments for components and PSU/cables. Clean look and better airflow.

Cooling Support

Air Cooling Support

Maximum CPU cooler height and number/rating of fan mounts.

Liquid Cooling Support

Radiator mount locations and maximum radiator thickness/length.

Fan Configurations

Standard fan sizes (120mm, 140mm) and mounting options.

Building Considerations

Component Clearance

Ensure the case fits your CPU cooler, GPU length, and other components.

Assembly Space

Consider interior space for building, especially your first few builds.

Weight and Portability

Consider if you need to move the case frequently.

Future Upgrades

Choose a case that accommodates potential future component upgrades.

Quick Facts

  • Case affects cooling performance
  • Size doesn't always equal performance
  • Airflow is more important than looks
  • Cable management improves airflow

Choosing a Case

  • Check motherboard compatibility
  • Consider component sizes
  • Think about cooling needs
  • Plan for future upgrades
  • Consider your space constraints
