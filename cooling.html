Cooling Systems

Keeping your components at optimal temperatures

Why is Cooling Important?

Computer components generate heat during operation, and excessive heat can cause performance degradation, system instability, and permanent damage. Proper cooling maintains optimal temperatures for performance and longevity. Modern CPUs and GPUs will automatically reduce performance (thermal throttling) when temperatures get too high to prevent damage.

Types of Cooling Solutions

Air Cooling

Uses metal heatsinks and fans to dissipate heat. Most common and reliable cooling method.

Tower Coolers

Large heatsinks with multiple heat pipes and fans. Excellent cooling performance for most users.

Low-Profile Coolers

Compact coolers for small cases. Good performance in small form factors.

Stock Coolers

Basic coolers included with CPUs. Adequate for basic use but limited for heavy workloads.

  • Reliable and low maintenance
  • Good performance for most users
  • No risk of leaks
  • Can be bulky

Liquid Cooling

Uses liquid coolant to transfer heat away from components. Better for high-performance systems.

AIO (All-in-One) Coolers

Pre-filled, sealed liquid cooling units. Easy to install and maintain.

Custom Loops

Custom-built liquid cooling systems. Best performance but complex and expensive.

  • Superior cooling performance
  • Quieter operation
  • Better aesthetics
  • More expensive and complex

Cooling Components

Heatsinks

Metal structures (usually aluminum or copper) that absorb and dissipate heat. Surface area and material affect performance.

Fans

Move air through heatsinks or across components. Size, speed, and airflow design matter.

  • 120mm: Standard size, good balance
  • 140mm: Quieter, more airflow
  • 80/92mm: Compact cases

Heat Pipes

Sealed pipes containing liquid that transfers heat efficiently from hot spots to heatsink fins.

Radiators

In liquid cooling, dissipates heat from the liquid to the air. Size and thickness affect performance.

Pumps

Circulate coolant in liquid cooling systems. Reliability is crucial.

Thermal Paste

Conductive material that fills microscopic gaps between CPU/GPU and cooler for better heat transfer.

Case Airflow Basics

Positive vs Negative Pressure

Positive Pressure

More intake fans than exhaust. Helps prevent dust buildup but may reduce heat exhaust.

Negative Pressure

More exhaust fans than intake. Better heat removal but can draw in dust through gaps.

Neutral Pressure

Balance of intake and exhaust. Good compromise between cooling and dust management.

Common Fan Configurations

Front Intake, Rear Exhaust

Most common setup, creates good airflow path across components.

Front/Bottom Intake, Top/Rear Exhaust

Excellent for cooling, uses natural hot air rise.

Push-Pull Configuration

Two fans on radiator (one pushing, one pulling) for maximum cooling.

Temperature Guidelines

Safe Operating Temperatures

  • CPU: 60-80°C under load
  • GPU: 70-85°C under load
  • VRAM: 80-95°C
  • VRM: 90-105°C

Warning Temperatures

  • CPU: 90°C+ (thermal throttling)
  • GPU: 90°C+ (performance drop)
  • Any Component: 100°C+ (danger zone)

Cooling Maintenance

Dust Cleaning

Clean fans and heatsinks every 3-6 months using compressed air or soft brush.

Thermal Paste Replacement

Replace every 2-3 years or if temperatures increase significantly.

Fan Maintenance

Check for unusual noises, ensure fans spin freely, replace if failing.

Liquid Cooling Care

Check for leaks, monitor pump operation, replace coolant every 2-3 years for custom loops.

Quick Facts

  • Cooling affects performance
  • Dust is the enemy of cooling
  • Liquid cooling isn't always better
  • Airflow direction matters

Choosing Cooling

  • Consider your CPU/GPU
  • Check case compatibility
  • Think about noise levels
  • Plan for maintenance

Troubleshooting

  • High temps: Check thermal paste
  • Noisy fans: Clean or replace
  • Poor airflow: Reorganize cables
  • System crashes: Check temps
